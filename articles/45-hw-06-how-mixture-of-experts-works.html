How Mixture-of-Experts Works

Part 6 of the How It Actually Works series
╌╌╌╌

A PM is evaluating providers & encounters a puzzling claim. DeepSeek V3 has 671 billion parameters — roughly the same scale as GPT-4 — yet it's dramatically cheaper to run. The benchmark scores are competitive. The latency is comparable. How does a model that's supposedly the same size cost so much less? The answer is Mixture-of-Experts, & it's the architectural trick that broke the assumption that bigger models must be more expensive models.

The Sparse Activation Trick

In a traditional "dense" model, every parameter participates in processing every token. If the model has 70 billion parameters, all 70 billion do work for each token that passes through. A Mixture-of-Experts model works differently. It splits the network's feed-forward layers — the biggest computational component — into many separate "expert" sub-networks. For each token, a lightweight router network decides which small subset of experts to activate. The rest sit idle.

DeepSeek V3 illustrates this concretely. It has 256 expert sub-networks but activates only 8 per token. So while the model's total knowledge is stored across 671 billion parameters, any given token only touches about 37 billion. The model has the capacity of a 671B-parameter network — it can store as much knowledge & nuance — but the compute cost of a 37B-parameter one. This is why it's cheap: you're paying for the compute of the active parameters, not the total parameters.

Mixtral followed a similar pattern at a smaller scale: 8 experts, 2 active per token. GPT-4 is widely believed to use MoE as well, though OpenAI hasn't confirmed the specifics. The pattern is consistent — the frontier of cost-efficient AI runs on sparse architectures.

The Router Is the Brain

The router network is small — just a linear layer that takes a token's representation & outputs a score for each expert. The top-K experts by score get activated. This happens at every layer of the network, & different tokens in the same sentence can route to entirely different experts. A token about Python syntax might activate different experts than a token about French grammar, even within the same response.

Training the router is tricky. Left unconstrained, the model tends to develop "expert collapse" — routing most tokens to just a few popular experts while the rest are underutilized. This wastes capacity. To prevent it, training includes load-balancing losses that penalize uneven expert utilization, encouraging the model to spread tokens across experts. DeepSeek V3 used an auxiliary-loss-free approach that's even more elegant — it adds a bias term to the routing scores that adjusts dynamically, nudging underused experts to receive more traffic without distorting the main training signal.

The experts don't specialize in the way you might expect. They're not neatly organized — one for math, one for poetry, one for code. The specialization emerges during training & tends to be more abstract, operating at the level of token patterns rather than human-recognizable domains. Attempting to interpret which expert "does what" is an active research area, but the honest answer is that the routing patterns are complex & not fully understood.

What This Means for the Market

MoE is the primary reason the cost-per-quality curve has dropped so steeply. Before MoE became mainstream, getting GPT-4-level quality meant paying GPT-4-level compute costs. With MoE, you get the quality that comes from storing knowledge across hundreds of billions of parameters while only paying to activate a fraction of them per token. The economics shifted from "quality requires proportional compute" to "quality requires proportional storage, but compute can be sparse."

For PMs evaluating models, this means parameter count alone tells you almost nothing about cost. A 671B MoE model can be cheaper to serve than a 70B dense model, cuz what matters is active parameters per token, not total parameters. When a provider quotes you a price, understanding whether the model is dense or MoE tells you a lot about whether that price has room to drop further — & whether a competitor could undercut it by simply using a sparser architecture. The model's size is no longer its cost. Its active size is.
