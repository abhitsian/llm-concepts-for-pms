The Physical World Doesn't Care

Part 10 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

The most grounded AI take of 2024 came from a CEO who moves burritos, not bytes.

DoorDash's Tony Xu, Q4 2024 earnings call:

"LLMs and AI agents don't necessarily play a significant role. They're an interface in some ways to the physical world, but they certainly don't solve the tasks in the physical world, such as providing the logistics infrastructure, understanding how to do it at the highest quality with the lowest cost, with the greatest accuracy."

Xu isn't dismissing AI. He's making a more precise claim: the hard part of DoorDash was never the software interface. It was the logistics network — the routing, the driver fleet, the merchant relationships, the last-mile economics. An LLM can't deliver a sandwich. It can help you order one faster, but the atoms still need to move.

Atoms Are Slow

Uber's Dara Khosrowshahi, echoing the same thesis:

"We don't operate in the virtual world, we operate in the physical world. You have to get the regulations up. You have to build the cars. You have to build the sensor stacks; the models have to get there."

On autonomous vehicles specifically: "Autonomous vehicles are likely to remain a very small portion of the rideshare category for many years to come." Khosrowshahi isn't scared of self-driving cars. He's pointing out that the physical world has friction that software can't abstract away — regulations, manufacturing, sensor hardware, weather, road conditions, human unpredictability.

On autonomous delivery, Xu was blunter: "It's mostly been filled with lots of pain and suffering." Years of investment. Marginal results. The physical world doesn't move at software speed, & every robotics startup that's tried to replace human delivery drivers has learned this the hard way.

AI in Your Pocket, AI in Your Cart

Apple's Tim Cook took the physical-world thesis in a different direction. On markets where Apple Intelligence had launched: "In the markets where we had launched Apple Intelligence, they outperformed the markets that we did not." Apple's AI strategy is fundamentally physical — tied to the silicon in your pocket, running on-device, respecting hardware constraints. While every other company is building cloud-first AI, Apple is building chip-first AI. The moat isn't the model. It's the A-series & M-series processors that run the model locally, privately, without a round trip to a data center.

Instacart's Fidji Simo revealed what AI looks like when it's embedded in physical retail:

"A simple 'Got everything you need?' prompt is driving a nearly one percentage point lift in basket size on average."

This is about Instacart's AI-powered Caper Carts — physical shopping carts with screens that use AI to suggest items as you shop. The value isn't the AI. It's the AI embedded in a physical cart, in a physical store, influencing physical purchases. One percentage point of basket size across thousands of stores is worth millions in incremental revenue. Not from a chatbot. From a shopping cart that asks if you forgot the milk.

The Atom Test

The pattern across these earnings calls reveals a clean dividing line in the AI disruption landscape. The companies with the deepest physical-world moats — logistics networks, hardware ecosystems, physical retail infrastructure — are the least worried about AI disruption & the most positioned to use AI as a multiplier. DoorDash uses AI to optimize routes, not replace drivers. Apple uses AI to sell more iPhones, not replace them. Instacart uses AI to increase basket sizes, not eliminate stores.

Meanwhile, the virtual-world companies got disrupted. Chegg. Stack Overflow's traffic. Digital-only content plays. The companies whose entire value chain existed in bits got compressed by a better bit-generator.

Your moat's durability depends on how many atoms are in it. If your product moves physical goods, operates physical infrastructure, or is embedded in physical hardware, AI is a tailwind. If your product is purely digital — esp if it's primarily an information-retrieval service — AI is an existential threat. The physical world doesn't care how smart the model gets. Somebody still has to deliver the sandwich.
