The Bionic Bar

Part 8 of Field Notes: AI Economics series
╌╌╌╌

Three speakers. Three independent classes. Three independent answers to the same question. Across the first four sessions of Apoorv Agrawal's Stanford AI Economics course, students kept asking variants of the same thing: what do you do with your career in a world where AI infrastructure capex is bigger than the Manhattan Project and the labor model of every company you might join is about to be rewritten?

None of the three speakers told them to start a company immediately. None told them to wait. All three told them the same thing in different words: stop optimizing for credentials, start optimizing for the rate at which you absorb new tools.

Brad Gerstner: Bionic, Not Branded

Gerstner, founder of Altimeter and one of the larger investors in OpenAI, Anthropic, and NVIDIA, was the guest speaker for the intro class. He framed it as a hiring change he is already implementing:

"In a lot of schools today, in particular colleges on the East Coast, people don't really know what to do with AI. I say all the time, you got to make yourself bionic with AI. You can't consume enough AI today. It used to be: you go to this school, you get a job at a place like Grok or Altimeter. Today, I really care where you went to school — I want somebody who shows up and delivers abnormal value. Bionic value."

The shift Gerstner is pointing at is structural. The pedigree premium that elite institutions sold for two generations is being repriced. The information asymmetry it monetized — access to people, frameworks, recruiting pipelines — is being commoditized by tools any 22-year-old can use from a coffee shop. What replaces pedigree is the demonstrable ability to deliver a 10x output with AI in the loop. Not "knows AI." Not "has used AI." Operates at multiples of unaugmented humans because the AI is integrated into how they think.

Chase Lochmiller: The Process Is the Asset

Lochmiller, the Crusoe CEO, was the guest in the data-center class a few weeks later. Asked by a student worried about whether they should start a company now or gain experience first, he answered with what he called Crusoe's core value:

"It's not the exact things you learn in school that matter. It's the process of learning. One of our core values at Crusoe is living on the infinite growth. Nobody's a finished product. Everybody's a work in progress. If you can get better, learn more, have that tenacity to improve every single day, over time you get this exponential compounding. The most valuable asset any of us can have."

This is closer to a craftsperson's model of growth than a careerist's. The compounding is not in promotions or in titles. It is in the gap between what you knew six months ago and what you know now — multiplied by however many six-month cycles you stack.

Ali Ghodsi: Calm Down

Ghodsi was the guest the following week, opening his fireside with a clear-eyed read of the room. He made the most counterintuitive case:

"You guys can chill out. Don't be stressed. I think times are crazy. The stress makes people do stupid things. Whatever happens to be the crazy thing everybody's talking about on Twitter — it makes people have tunnel vision and not work on the right stuff. The last two years, interns have come in — instead of asking for career advice, they're 22-year-olds saying 'oh my god, should I start my own company and be a CEO? If I delay by six months, have I ruined my career, AGI is going to happen, I'm going to miss the boat?'"

His pushback: things take time. AGI may already be here, but the deployment of AGI through the world's organizations will take years. The careers built during that deployment are made of concrete work, not Twitter takes. The students worrying about missing the boat are exactly the ones most likely to make decisions they regret.

What Stuck

The synthesis across three pretty different operators: bionic value over pedigree (Gerstner), process over outcome (Lochmiller), patience over panic (Ghodsi). The common thread is what they did not say. None of them said "go to grad school." None of them said "join a big-name company." None of them said "raise a seed and figure it out." All three pointed at the same place: your daily rate of compounding, augmented by the best tools available, applied to a real problem with real organizational context. Everything else is noise.
