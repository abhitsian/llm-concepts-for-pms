It Starts with Sand

Module 1 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

Silicon is the second most abundant element on earth. It's in sand, in rock, in the ground under your feet. Taken raw, it does nothing useful. But when you purify it — and we mean purify it to a degree that is almost absurd, 99.9999999% pure, nine nines — it becomes a semiconductor. Something that conducts electricity sometimes, and doesn't other times.

That "sometimes" is the entire foundation of the digital world.

The Switch

The ability to switch electrical current on and off is a binary decision. On = 1. Off = 0. One switch is called a transistor. A modern AI chip has 92 billion of them, each smaller than a virus, switching on and off billions of times per second. All of computation — every calculation, every word an AI generates, every photo that loads on your phone — is just billions of these tiny switches flipping in coordinated patterns.

There is no magic in a computer. There is no intelligence in a chip. There is only a vast number of switches that flip very fast, in patterns designed by humans. When someone tells you that AI "thinks" — what they mean is that 92 billion transistors are switching on and off in a sequence that produces output indistinguishable from thought. The distinction matters, because it grounds everything that follows in physics rather than metaphor.

Why This Is Hard

Making transistors that small requires manufacturing at a scale of precision that has no analogy in everyday life. The current leading-edge process — 2 nanometers — means etching features that are roughly 10 atoms wide. Imagine shrinking New York City until the Empire State Building is the width of a human hair, and then building perfectly functional plumbing inside every building in that miniaturized city. That's the challenge.

The machines that do this use extreme ultraviolet light — a wavelength shorter than anything in sunlight — to etch circuit patterns onto silicon wafers. The light is generated by shooting a tiny droplet of molten tin with a laser, 50,000 times per second, creating a plasma that emits the exact wavelength needed. This sounds like science fiction. It is the most precise manufacturing process humans have ever devised, running at industrial scale, 24 hours a day.

Why This Matters for AI

Every previous technology shift — internet, mobile, cloud — was fundamentally a software story. You could understand the implications without understanding the hardware. Dell made a box, you bought it, software ran on it.

AI is different. For the first time since the invention of the transistor, the hardware is the story. What chips exist, how many there are, where they are, who controls them — these determine which AI capabilities are possible, how much they cost, and who wins. A product manager who doesn't understand this is flying blind in a way that a PM in 2005 could safely ignore.

The rest of this course starts here, with sand, because the constraints that matter most in AI are physical. You cannot write more code and get more AI. The bottleneck is atoms — silicon, copper, water for cooling, electricity, land. Understanding this prevents the magical thinking that makes most AI strategies useless.

First Principles Takeaway

AI capability is physically constrained. The ceiling on what AI can do at any given moment is set not by software cleverness but by how many transistors exist, how fast they switch, and how much power they consume. Everything that follows in this course — chips, clusters, training, inference, cost curves, product decisions — traces back to this physical reality.
