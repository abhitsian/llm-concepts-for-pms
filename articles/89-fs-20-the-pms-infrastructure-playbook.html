The Five Questions for Every AI Feature

Before building any AI feature, run it through these five questions. Each connects directly to a layer of the physical stack:

1. "What happens when the model gets 10x better and 10x cheaper in 18 months?"

The 280x cost collapse (Module 11) tells you costs fall roughly 10x every 18 months. If your feature's value proposition survives a 10x better, 10x cheaper model — because you built the workflow, the data loop, and the integration — build it. If a 10x better model makes your feature unnecessary because you were just wrapping the model — rethink it. The durable products use AI to build something AI alone can't replace: trust, workflow depth, organizational context.

2. "Which model tier does this feature actually need?"

The cost-capability-speed triangle (Module 10) means you should never use one model for everything. FAQ lookups don't need a frontier model. Complex multi-step reasoning does. Model routing — sending easy queries to cheap models and hard queries to expensive models — is a product architecture decision that directly determines your margin. 80% of queries may not need your most expensive model. Design the routing logic as carefully as you design the user experience.

3. "How long is my system prompt, and does it need to be?"

System prompt length has a direct cost impact (Module 10). A 5,000-token system prompt costs ~10x more per query on prefill than a 500-token one. Every instruction, every few-shot example, every context document in your prompt is money per query. Audit your system prompts ruthlessly. Move static instructions to fine-tuning. Use prompt caching for content that doesn't change between users. This isn't engineering optimization — it's product economics.

4. "What's my context window budget per session?"

Context length is the most expensive product decision most PMs don't realize they're making (Module 5). Passing full conversation history vs. a compressed summary can be a 50x cost difference. Analyze how much context your feature actually needs. For most enterprise features, a well-designed summary of the last 5 turns is as effective as passing 50 turns verbatim — at 1/10th the cost.

5. "Am I building a feature or a workflow?"

Single-turn AI features (summarize this, answer that) are commoditizing fast (Module 12). Any foundation model can do them. The defensible product is the workflow: multi-step processes that integrate with systems, maintain state, handle approvals, and manage exceptions. Features are table stakes. Workflows are moats.

The Infrastructure Calendar

AI capability doesn't improve smoothly. It arrives in discrete jumps tied to the physical stack:

Quarterly: New model versions from labs (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google). These bring capability improvements and usually cost reductions. Review your model selection and routing quarterly.

Semi-annually: New GPU generations ship in volume. H100 → H200 → B200 → B300. Each generation brings 2–3x inference improvement. Plan feature launches to coincide with new hardware availability — the cost of your feature drops when new GPUs reach production scale.

Annually: New serving software releases (vLLM, TensorRT major versions). These bring 2–5x efficiency improvements through better batching, caching, and optimization. Re-benchmark your inference costs annually against the latest serving stack.

Every 2–3 years: New data center capacity comes online. This expands total AI supply, reduces queuing and rate limits, and enables new scale-dependent features. If your feature was rate-limited at launch, revisit it when capacity expands.

What Not to Build

Don't build a model wrapper. If your product is "ChatGPT/Claude but for [vertical]" with no proprietary data, no workflow integration, and no distribution advantage — you will be commoditized. The foundation model providers are moving up the stack. They will build your product and give it away to drive API usage.

Don't assume current cost structures are permanent. If your business model depends on AI being expensive (you're the premium provider in a world of expensive AI), the 280x cost curve will destroy that positioning. Build for the world where AI is cheap and abundant, not the world where it's expensive and scarce.

Don't ignore the physical constraints. If your roadmap assumes unlimited AI scaling with no capacity constraints, you haven't understood the stack. There are moments — new chip launches, data center openings, power contract activations — when capacity expands. Between those moments, capacity is fixed. Your product should handle graceful degradation when AI resources are constrained.

The One-Page Mental Model

The physical world creates the ceiling. ASML makes the machines. TSMC makes the chips. The power grid limits how many run. None of this is software.

The software determines how close you get to the ceiling. CUDA, vLLM, custom kernels. Invisible to users, determinative of cost and performance.

The model is the intelligence. Trained once, expensive, getting better fast, getting cheaper fast. Not your moat.

Inference is the meter running. Every query costs money. Every agent step costs more. The cost falls 280x over 3 years. Build for where the economics will be.

The application layer is where you live — and where commoditization risk is highest. Your moat exists in what models can't easily replicate: workflow, data, trust, integration.

The agent shift is the strategy. Not query-answer. Goal-action. The companies that build the agent orchestration layer own the next decade.

The window is narrow. The stack below is consolidating fast. Your advantage is position, not technology. Use it while it's yours.

It all starts with sand. It ends with what you build on top of it.
