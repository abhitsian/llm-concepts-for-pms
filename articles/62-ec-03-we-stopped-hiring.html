We Stopped Hiring

Part 3 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

No earnings call quote in 2024 landed harder than Sebastian Siemiatkowski's flat, matter-of-fact statement about what Klarna had done to its workforce. No hedging. No "we're exploring efficiencies." Just the numbers:

"We stopped hiring about a year ago. We were 4,500, now we're 3,500."

A thousand ppl gone through attrition alone. No layoff announcement. No restructuring memo. They just stopped replacing anyone who left — & let AI absorb the work. Klarna's AI chatbot, built on OpenAI's models, was handling the equivalent of 700 full-time customer service agents in its first month of deployment. By mid-2024, that number had climbed to 853 agent-equivalents. Then Siemiatkowski went further than any CEO had gone before:

"AI is capable of doing all our jobs, my own included. Because our work is simply reasoning combined with knowledge and experience. And the most critical breakthrough, reasoning, is behind us."

The Reversal Nobody Talks About

But here's the part that didn't make the headlines. Quietly, in 2025, Klarna started rehiring human customer service agents. The AI chatbot was fast & cheap — but quality had dropped. Complex cases got mishandled. Customer satisfaction on AI-resolved tickets lagged behind human-resolved ones. The most vocal AI-replacement story in tech history had to walk it back.

This is the most complete cautionary arc in corporate AI: fire humans, let AI handle it, watch quality drop, rehire humans. Not cuz AI is useless — it handled the high-volume, low-complexity tickets brilliantly. But cuz the last 20% of customer service cases require judgment, empathy, & the ability to deviate from the script. AI is great at the 80%. It struggles with the 20% that determines whether customers stay or leave.

The Productivity Multiplier

Duolingo's Luis von Ahn offered a different framing — not replacement, but multiplication. In an internal memo that went public, he declared Duolingo would be "AI-first" & began offboarding roughly 10% of its contract workforce. But the key insight wasn't in the cuts:

"With the same number of people, we can make four or five times as much content."

This is the version of the AI labor story that's harder to argue against. Duolingo didn't fire everyone & replace them with bots. It kept its core team & made each person dramatically more productive. Fewer contractors, yes — but the remaining ppl weren't doing the same job. They were doing a bigger job, faster. The headcount shrank but the output exploded.

The CEOs Who Said It Plainly

JPMorgan's Jamie Dimon didn't sugarcoat it either. On Bloomberg:

"It will eliminate jobs. People should stop sticking their heads in the sand."

Dimon runs a company with 310,000 employees. When he says AI will eliminate jobs, he's not talking about someone else's workforce. He's talking about his own. And he's telling you in advance, on the record, that it's coming.

Uber's Dara Khosrowshahi put a timeline on it — & it's not comforting:

"AI will be able to replace the work that 70% to 80% of humans can do. Ten years is not a lot of time for society to adjust."

The man running the largest gig-work platform on earth is saying that most of the work humans do today will be automatable within a decade. Not "could be." Will be. And that society isn't ready.

For PMs, the pattern across these quotes isn't "AI replaces everyone." It's more nuanced & more important: AI collapses the bottom of the labor stack first — the routine, repeatable, rule-based work — & pushes the remaining humans up into judgment, creativity, & exception-handling. Klarna learned the hard way that you can't automate the exceptions. Duolingo learned you can multiply the humans instead of replacing them. Dimon & Khosrowshahi are simply telling you the direction — esp if you're building products that employ, manage, or serve large workforces. The workforce question isn't "if." It's how fast, & what's left when the routine work is gone.
