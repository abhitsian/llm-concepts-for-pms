How Distillation Works

The idea is simple. You have a large, expensive "teacher" model that's very good at some task. You want a small, cheap "student" model that's nearly as good. Instead of training the student on raw data — the way you'd train any model from scratch — you train it on the teacher's outputs. The student learns not just the right answers, but the teacher's distribution over all possible answers — the full probability landscape, including which wrong answers are almost right & which are way off.

This is the key insight. When a teacher model processes a math problem, its output isn't just "42." It's a probability distribution: 42 has 85% probability, 41 has 8%, 43 has 5%, & everything else shares the remaining 2%. That distribution contains information that the raw correct answer alone doesn't — it encodes the teacher's uncertainty, the relationships between similar answers, the structure of the problem space. Training on these "soft labels" transfers knowledge far more efficiently than training on hard correct/incorrect labels from a dataset.

The result: a 7B-parameter student trained via distillation can match or beat a 70B-parameter model trained from scratch on the same benchmarks. The student inherits a compressed version of the teacher's reasoning patterns without needing the teacher's size. It's like an apprentice learning a craft by watching a master — the apprentice doesn't need to rediscover every technique independently. They learn the refined version directly.

Why This Broke the Economics

Before distillation went mainstream, building a good model meant assembling a massive dataset, buying thousands of GPUs, & spending months (and tens or hundreds of millions of dollars) training from scratch. The capital barrier was the moat. Only a handful of organizations — OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, Meta — could play the game, cuz only they had the resources to pretrain frontier models.

Distillation changed the entry requirements. You don't need to pretrain a frontier model yourself — you need access to one's outputs. Run a million prompts through a powerful teacher, collect its responses (with their probability distributions), & use that as training data for a smaller model. The cost drops from nine figures to six. The timeline drops from months to weeks. The GPU requirement drops from thousands to dozens.

DeepSeek demonstrated this at scale with R1. They trained the full R1 model with reinforcement learning — an expensive, complex process. Then they distilled R1's reasoning capabilities into a family of smaller models ranging from 1.5B to 70B parameters. These distilled models outperformed comparably-sized models that were trained without the benefit of a reasoning teacher. The expensive RL training happened once. The distillation step was comparatively cheap, & it produced a whole lineup of models.

The Moat Question

This has direct consequences for how you think about competitive advantage. If anyone can distill a frontier model's capabilities into a smaller, cheaper model, the frontier model itself isn't a durable moat. OpenAI trains GPT-5. Within weeks, ppl are distilling its reasoning patterns into open-weight models that run on a single GPU. The lead that cost billions to develop gets partially replicated for a fraction of the cost.

The moat moves up the stack. It's not the model — it's the data flywheel that trains better teachers over time. It's the user base generating feedback that improves alignment. It's the product surface that makes the model useful in a specific domain. It's the integrations, the trust, the workflow fit that keep users coming back even when a cheaper model appears.

For PMs, the strategic takeaway is stark: if your product's value proposition is "we use a good model," distillation ensures that advantage has a short half-life. The model is the commodity. Everything you build around it — esp the things that get better with usage — is where durable differentiation lives.
