How DeepSeek Trained R1 for $5.5M

Part 9 of the How It Actually Works series
╌╌╌╌

In January 2025, the AI world woke up to a paper from a Chinese hedge fund's research lab. DeepSeek R1 matched OpenAI's o1 on major reasoning benchmarks — math, code, science — at a reported training cost of $5.5 million. For context, estimates for training GPT-4 range from $50M to $100M+. The industry narrative that frontier models required frontier budgets cracked overnight. Here's how they did it.

Architecture That Cheats the Math

R1 is built on DeepSeek-V3, which uses a Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture. The model has 671 billion total parameters, but only 37 billion are active for any given token. Think of it as a building with 256 specialist offices — for each query, a routing mechanism sends the work to just 8 of them. You get the knowledge capacity of a 671B model with the compute cost of a 37B one. This alone slashes training & inference costs dramatically compared to dense models where every parameter fires on every token.

Then there's Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) — DeepSeek's custom attention mechanism. Standard transformers store key-value pairs for every token in context, & this KV-cache grows linearly with sequence length. MLA compresses keys & values into a low-dimensional latent space before storing them, cutting memory usage by roughly 90%. This means you can train with longer contexts on fewer GPUs, & at inference time you can serve more users per machine. It's an architectural innovation that converts directly into dollars saved.

Reinforcement Learning Without the Bloat

The reasoning ability in R1 comes from reinforcement learning — the model learns to think step-by-step by being rewarded for correct answers. The standard approach (PPO, used by OpenAI) requires training a separate reward model that's often as large as the policy model itself. That's two massive models in GPU memory during training. DeepSeek used GRPO — Group Relative Policy Optimization — which eliminates the reward model entirely. Instead, it generates multiple answers for each problem, scores them against ground-truth, & uses the relative ranking within each group as the training signal. Same quality of learning signal, half the memory footprint.

What made R1 genuinely surprising was what emerged from this RL training. The model spontaneously developed reasoning behaviors — self-verification, backtracking, breaking problems into sub-steps — without being explicitly taught any of these patterns. DeepSeek called this the model's "aha moment." The RL objective just said "get the right answer," & the model discovered structured reasoning as the strategy to do it.

Hardware Constraints as a Forcing Function

DeepSeek trained on NVIDIA H800 GPUs — the export-restricted chips that are weaker than the H100s available to American labs. Lower memory bandwidth, slower interconnects. This constraint forced every architectural decision toward efficiency. FP8 mixed precision training — using 8-bit floating point for most operations — squeezed more compute out of each chip. The MoE architecture meant less communication between GPUs cuz only a fraction of parameters needed updating per step. The $5.5M figure is compute cost alone — it doesn't include researcher salaries, failed experiments, or the R&D that produced V3 as a foundation — but the raw efficiency is still remarkable.

The final move was distillation. DeepSeek took R1's reasoning ability & distilled it into smaller models ranging from 1.5B to 70B parameters. The distilled models inherited much of the reasoning capability at a fraction of the serving cost. The 32B distilled model, esp, hit a sweet spot — strong enough for most reasoning tasks, small enough to run on modest hardware.

For PMs, the DeepSeek story reframes the entire competitive landscape. The moat in AI isn't how much compute you can afford — it's how cleverly you use what you have. A team with architectural insight & hardware constraints produced a model that embarrassed organizations spending 10x more. This means the frontier isn't stable. Any lab with a genuine architectural innovation can leapfrog incumbents, & the cost of "good enough" reasoning is dropping faster than anyone's pricing models assumed. Plan accordingly: the model you're paying premium prices for today will have a cheaper equivalent sooner than your contract renewal date.
