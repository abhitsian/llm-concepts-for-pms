Before Asking for Headcount, Prove AI Can't

Part 4 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

In April 2025, Shopify CEO Tobi Lutke published an internal memo on X that quietly became the most referenced management directive in tech. Not cuz it was revolutionary — but cuz it said, in writing, what dozens of CEOs were already thinking but hadn't formalized:

"Reflexive AI usage is now a baseline expectation at Shopify."

"Before asking for more headcount and resources, teams must demonstrate why they cannot get what they want done using AI."

Read that second line again. The burden of proof has flipped. It used to be: "We need more ppl to do more work." Now it's: "Prove that AI can't do this work before we give you more ppl." Headcount is no longer the default answer to a resourcing problem. AI is. Humans are the exception that requires justification.

The Salesforce Playbook

Marc Benioff took this further — not as a memo, but as a math problem. On the Logan Bartlett Show in September 2025, he laid out the numbers with zero ambiguity:

"I've reduced it from 9,000 heads to about 5,000, because I need less heads."

Four thousand customer service jobs, gone. Support costs down 17%. Agentforce — Salesforce's AI agent platform — doing the work. And Benioff wasn't apologetic about it. By Q3 FY2025, the pitch to enterprise customers had shifted entirely: six of Salesforce's top ten deals in the quarter were companies that wanted to "transform with Agentforce." The pricing model? Roughly $2 per conversation. Not per seat. Not per license. Per conversation.

This is the economic shift that makes the Lutke memo inevitable. When the cost of AI-handled work drops to $2 per interaction, the math on a $70,000-per-year customer service agent becomes impossible to defend — esp for the routine, repeatable queries that make up the bulk of any support operation. You don't need a mandate from the CEO to see where this goes. The spreadsheet tells you.

The Consumer's Advantage

But not every company is building AI infrastructure. Some are just using it. And that distinction matters enormously for the P&L. Airbnb's Brian Chesky made this point with a clarity that should resonate with every PM at a non-AI-native company:

"Our investment in AI will not affect the P&L. I don't think you'll see it in the P&L. We do not have the huge CapEx cost base."

Airbnb isn't training foundation models. It isn't building data centers. It's calling APIs. The company gets the productivity benefits of AI — better search, smarter customer support, automated content moderation — without the billions in capital expenditure that Microsoft, Google, & Meta are pouring into infrastructure. Chesky is saying: we are a consumer of AI, not a builder of it. And consumers get the margin.

This is the split that's emerging across the entire economy. On one side: companies spending $10-50 billion per year on AI infrastructure (the hyperscalers, the chip makers, the foundation model labs). On the other: companies spending a rounding error on API calls & getting 80% of the productivity gains. The infrastructure builders are making a bet that the market will be enormous. The infrastructure consumers — like Airbnb, like Shopify — are already capturing the value without making that bet.

For PMs, the Lutke memo is the new operating system. If you're building a product roadmap that starts with "we need to hire X more engineers," you'd better have a damn good answer for why AI can't do part of that work. Not all of it. Probably not even most of it. But if you haven't even asked the question — if AI usage isn't reflexive, as Lutke put it — you're already behind. The companies that will win the next five years aren't the ones that spend the most on AI. They're the ones that reorganize their operating model around it — making AI the first option, not the last resort, & keeping headcount for the work that genuinely requires human judgment.
