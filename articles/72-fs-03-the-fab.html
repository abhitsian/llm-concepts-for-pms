The Fab

Module 3 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

The EUV machines go into semiconductor fabs — fabrication plants. The most important one is TSMC, the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company. TSMC makes roughly 90% of the world's leading-edge chips. Apple's iPhone chips. Nvidia's AI GPUs. AMD's processors. Google's TPUs. They don't design chips — they manufacture them. Their job is to take someone else's design and physically make it in silicon, with near-perfect accuracy, at massive scale.

The Numbers That Matter

Building a new leading-edge fab costs $20 billion and takes four years. You can't just decide to build more AI chips and have them next quarter. The supply chain commitment starts today for chips that will run AI models in 2028.

This four-year lag is one of the most important facts in technology right now. The hyperscalers — Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta — are placing physical bets today on what AI demand will look like in 2028. They are buying turbines, reserving fab capacity, signing power purchase agreements, all for a future that hasn't arrived yet. When analysts talk about $600 billion in annual CapEx from the big four, most of that money is for infrastructure that won't be operational for 2–3 years.

Yield: The Only Metric

A fab's success is measured by yield — the percentage of chips that come off the production line working correctly. A 2-nanometer wafer contains hundreds of individual chip dies. If a single speck of dust lands on the wafer during manufacturing, every die it touches is ruined. The manufacturing environment must be 10,000 times cleaner than a hospital operating room.

TSMC's yield advantage is measured in single-digit percentage points — but those points translate to billions of dollars. If TSMC gets 85% yield where Intel gets 75%, TSMC produces ~13% more working chips from the same number of wafers. At the scale of hundreds of thousands of AI chips, that margin is the difference between profit and loss for the entire AI hardware ecosystem.

The Allocation Game

TSMC doesn't sell chips to everyone equally. It allocates fab capacity — and the most important negotiation in the AI industry happens years in advance, when companies bid for future production slots. Nvidia secured TSMC's most advanced capacity early and aggressively. This is partly why Nvidia dominates: not just better chip design, but locked-in manufacturing access that competitors can't easily match.

Apple is TSMC's largest customer by revenue. Nvidia is growing fast. When demand for AI chips surges, someone gets squeezed out. The allocation game is zero-sum: every wafer that makes AI chips is a wafer that doesn't make smartphone chips or automotive chips. The decisions TSMC makes about allocation ripple through every industry that depends on advanced semiconductors.

The Reshoring Problem

The concentration of leading-edge manufacturing in Taiwan — a 180-mile-wide island across the strait from mainland China — is a strategic vulnerability that everyone acknowledges and nobody has solved. The U.S. CHIPS Act allocated $52 billion to build domestic fabs. TSMC is building in Arizona. Intel is building in Ohio. Samsung is building in Texas.

But there's a catch: it costs 30–50% more to manufacture chips in the U.S. than in Taiwan, primarily due to labor costs, construction costs, and the lack of an established semiconductor supply chain ecosystem. The new Arizona fab has faced delays and cost overruns. Building the fab is hard; building the ecosystem around it — the specialized chemical suppliers, the skilled technicians, the institutional knowledge — is harder.

First Principles Takeaway

The fab is where design meets reality. A brilliant chip architecture is worthless without the manufacturing capability to produce it at scale and yield. The 4-year build cycle for new fabs means today's CapEx decisions determine AI capability in 2028–2030. When you hear "AI will scale exponentially" — check whether the fabs exist to make it happen. Physics doesn't bend to forecast slides.
