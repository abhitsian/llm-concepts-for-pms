The Fab

Module 3 of From Sand to Software

The ASML machines go into semiconductor fabs — fabrication plants. The most important one belongs to TSMC, the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company. TSMC makes roughly 90% of the world's leading-edge chips. Apple's iPhone processors. Nvidia's H100 and B200 AI GPUs. AMD's chips. Google's TPUs. Qualcomm's mobile chips. They don't design any of these — they manufacture them. TSMC's job is to take someone else's design and physically make it in silicon, with near-perfect accuracy, at massive scale.

The Four-Year Lag

Building a new leading-edge fab costs $20 billion and takes four years. You can't just decide to build more AI chips and have them next quarter. The supply chain commitment starts today for chips that will run AI models in 2028–2030.

This four-year lag is one of the most important facts in technology right now. When Dylan Patel tracks the AI buildout at SemiAnalysis, he's literally watching from satellite imagery: concrete being poured, steel going up, turbines being delivered. The hyperscalers — Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta — are placing physical bets today on what AI demand will look like in 2028. They're buying turbines, reserving fab capacity, signing 20-year power purchase agreements, all for a future that hasn't arrived yet.

When analysts talk about $600+ billion in combined CapEx from the big four this year, most of that money is for infrastructure that won't be operational for 2–3 years. These aren't PowerPoint commitments. You don't sign a $2 billion turbine deposit on a whim. You don't negotiate a 20-year grid connection because you're following a trend.

Yield: The Only Metric That Matters

A fab's success is measured by yield — the percentage of chips that come off the line working correctly. A single wafer contains hundreds of chip dies. If a speck of dust lands during manufacturing, every die it touches is ruined. The environment must be 10,000 times cleaner than a hospital operating room.

TSMC's yield advantage is measured in single-digit percentage points. But those points translate to billions. If TSMC gets 85% yield where a competitor gets 75%, TSMC produces ~13% more working chips from the same wafers. At the volumes the AI industry demands — hundreds of thousands of chips per quarter — that margin is the difference between profit and loss for the entire hardware ecosystem.

The Allocation Game

TSMC doesn't sell chips to everyone equally. It allocates fab capacity — and the most important negotiation in the AI industry happens years in advance. Nvidia secured TSMC's most advanced capacity early and aggressively. This is partly why Nvidia dominates: not just better chip design, but locked-in manufacturing access that competitors can't match on short notice.

Apple is TSMC's largest customer by revenue. Nvidia is growing fast. When demand surges, someone gets squeezed. The allocation game is zero-sum: every wafer making AI chips is a wafer not making smartphone chips or automotive chips. The decisions TSMC makes about allocation ripple through every industry that depends on advanced semiconductors.

The Reshoring Problem

The concentration in Taiwan — a 180-mile-wide island across the strait from mainland China — is a strategic vulnerability everyone acknowledges and nobody has solved. The U.S. CHIPS Act allocated $52 billion to build domestic fabs. TSMC is building in Arizona. Intel is building in Ohio.

But it costs 30–50% more to manufacture chips in the U.S. than in Taiwan. The Arizona fab has faced delays and cost overruns. Building the fab is hard; building the ecosystem around it — specialized chemical suppliers, skilled technicians, the institutional knowledge accumulated over 40 years in Hsinchu — is harder. You can build the factory. You can't easily transplant the culture.

The takeaway for product leaders: AI capability has a four-year manufacturing pipeline. When your CEO asks "why can't we just get more GPU capacity?" — the answer traces back to decisions TSMC made about fab allocation in 2022. Physics doesn't bend to quarterly roadmaps.
