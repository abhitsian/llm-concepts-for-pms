The 280x Collapse

Module 11 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

Inference costs have fallen roughly 280-fold since late 2022. What cost $28 to run then costs $0.10 today for equivalent computation. This is not a typo. This is the single most important number in AI product strategy, and most product managers don't know it.

Where the 280x Came From

No single factor explains it. It's the compound effect of improvements across every layer:

Better chips (~10x) — Three GPU generations in three years (A100 → H100 → B200), each delivering 2–3x more performance per dollar.

Quantization (~4x) — FP32 → FP8 and beyond, running models at lower precision with minimal quality loss.

Software optimization (~10–23x) — vLLM's PagedAttention reduced memory waste from 80% to 4%. Continuous batching improved throughput by up to 23x. Speculative decoding added another 2–3x.

Architecture improvements (~4–8x) — Mixture-of-experts activates only a fraction of parameters per query. Distilled models deliver 80–90% of frontier quality at 10% the cost.

Competition + scale — More data centers, more providers, more supply. Prices drop when supply expands.

Hardware alone would have given ~10x. Software alone ~10x. Together with architecture and competition: 280x. And the curve hasn't flattened.

The Product Roadmap Signal

Features that seem unviable today will be viable in 12–18 months. An agentic workflow costing $2 per session today will cost $0.20 in 18 months. If you wait for the economics to make sense before building, you'll be 18 months behind the competitors who built now and scaled when costs dropped. The companies that bet on cost curves in previous transitions — cloud in 2008, streaming video in 2012, mobile apps in 2010 — were right every time.

Long-context features are moving from luxury to standard. Full conversation history, large document analysis — previously prohibitively expensive, now approaching economic viability. Design for them now.

Don't price based on today's costs. If you set AI feature pricing using current inference economics, you'll be overcharging in a year and competitors will undercut you. Price based on where costs will be in 12–18 months.

The Jevons Paradox

When something gets cheaper, people use more of it, not less. This is playing out in AI with mathematical precision. Google processes 20x more AI tokens now than a year ago — 480 trillion monthly to 1.3 quadrillion. Total AI compute demand is growing faster than costs are falling. The industry is running to stand still — building massive infrastructure just to keep up with demand that the falling costs themselves create.

Jensen Huang frames it as "compute equals GDP." The amount of AI inference a country can run is becoming a measure of economic capacity. Sam Altman calls inference tokens "the most valuable commodity on earth." Whether or not you believe the rhetoric, the Jevons math is clear: cheaper AI = more AI usage = more infrastructure needed = more CapEx = the buildout continues.

For product managers: AI usage will be far higher than you project. Users who interact with AI once a day at current prices will interact 10 times a day when it's 10x cheaper. Features that seem niche at current costs become mainstream at future costs. Plan for 10x the usage you see today.

The Takeaway

The 280x collapse is the most reliable signal in AI strategy. It says: build the workflow now, because the economics will catch up. The durable winners in every previous cost-curve transition were the companies that built the user experience and locked in distribution while the technology was still expensive, then rode the cost curve to profitability. The same pattern is playing out in AI, faster than any previous transition.
