The Application Layer

Module 12 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

Everything we've covered — sand to silicon, chips to clusters, training to inference — exists to serve this layer. The application layer is where users actually touch AI. It's where you build products. And it's the layer with the highest commoditization risk.

The Model Is Commoditizing

GPT-4 was state of the art in early 2023. A year later, open-source models of comparable capability were freely available. DeepSeek built a GPT-4 class model for a fraction of the cost. Meta gives Llama away for free. Google, Anthropic, OpenAI, and a dozen others all have frontier-class models. The frontier keeps advancing, but the distance between frontier and commodity is shrinking with every generation.

When a technology layer commoditizes, the value shifts to whatever is around it. Not the water — the pipes and the pump. Marc Benioff said it plainly: "All these large language models are the same. We just want the lowest cost one." He's not entirely right — meaningful differences exist between frontier models. But the direction is correct. The model is becoming a component, not a product.

Where Value Actually Lives

If the model commoditizes, what doesn't? Five things:

1. Workflow depth. Products that are woven into how work actually gets done — not just answering questions but executing multi-step processes, handling approvals, managing state across sessions. The deeper the workflow integration, the harder it is to replace, regardless of which model powers it.

2. Proprietary data. Models are trained on public data. Your competitive advantage is private data — customer interactions, ticket histories, workflow patterns, domain-specific knowledge — that no foundation model has seen. This data, used to fine-tune or provide context, creates a capability gap competitors can't close by switching models.

3. Distribution. Microsoft ships AI to 400M+ Office users through automatic updates. Apple embeds AI in 2 billion devices. Salesforce integrates into existing CRM workflows. Distribution beats model quality when the model is "good enough" — and the gap between best and good-enough is narrowing.

4. Trust. Enterprise customers don't switch AI providers because a benchmark improved 3%. They buy from vendors they trust with their data, their workflows, their compliance requirements. Trust takes years to build and seconds to lose. It's the ultimate non-commoditizable asset.

5. Integration breadth. The number of systems your product connects to, the depth of those connections, the years of API development and partner relationships — this creates switching costs that model improvements can't overcome. A company using Salesforce Agentforce doesn't switch because Claude 4 is slightly better than GPT-5. They switch when someone offers a better-integrated system.

The Agent Shift

The current paradigm is: user asks, AI answers. Query in, response out.

The emerging paradigm is: user states a goal, AI takes multiple actions over time to achieve it. The AI searches, reads files, calls APIs, executes code, sends messages, and makes decisions — autonomously, in sequence, until the job is done.

This changes everything. The UX shifts from "conversation interface" to "work management interface" — you assign tasks, monitor progress, review outputs, handle exceptions. The economics shift: an agent solving a complex task might generate 50x more tokens than a single-turn answer. But the value delivered — 3 hours of human work automated — makes the $2 inference cost irrelevant.

The products that matter in 3 years won't be "AI-assisted." They'll be AI-operated, with humans supervising, approving, and handling the exceptions that agents can't.

The Window

Every layer of this stack — from ASML's machines to TSMC's fabs to Nvidia's chips to the foundation model labs — is consolidating around a small number of winners. The application layer is still contested. Glean, Microsoft Copilot, Salesforce Agentforce, ServiceNow Now Assist, and dozens of startups are all racing to own the enterprise AI workflow.

The advantage for incumbents is position: they already own the workflow, the data, and the customer relationship. The advantage for startups is speed: they can build agent-native products without legacy constraints. The race is between position and velocity, and the window to establish dominance is narrowing as the stack below stabilizes.

The Complete Picture

From sand to software, the AI stack is a physical system with software running on top. Understanding where you sit in this stack — and which layers are commoditizing versus consolidating — is the most important strategic question for any product leader building with AI.

The layers below you set the ceiling: chip supply, memory bandwidth, power availability, inference cost. The layers around you determine your moat: workflow depth, data, distribution, trust. And the cost curve beneath it all is falling 280x every three years, making today's impossible features tomorrow's table stakes.

Build for where the economics will be. Build the workflow, not the wrapper. And never forget that it all starts with sand.
