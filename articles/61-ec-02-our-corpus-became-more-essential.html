Our Corpus Became More Essential

Part 2 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

The conventional AI disruption narrative goes like this: model gets trained on your data, model replaces your product, you die. Chegg lived it. Stack Overflow nearly lived it. But Reddit — the largest repository of authentic human conversation on the internet — flipped the script entirely. AI didn't kill Reddit. It made Reddit's data more valuable than it had ever been.

The $203 Million Insight

When Reddit filed its S-1 in early 2024, buried in the revenue disclosures was a number that rewired how ppl thought about data & AI: $203 million in data licensing deals, primarily with Google. Reddit had been selling access to its corpus — two decades of human-written posts, comments, arguments, reviews, confessions, & advice — to the companies training the next generation of language models.

By Q2 2025, CEO Steve Huffman wasn't being coy about the leverage this gave them. On the earnings call, he laid it out plainly:

"Every variable has changed since we signed those first deals. Our corpus is bigger, it's more distinct, more essential. And so of course, this puts us in a really good strategic position."

The word essential is doing a lot of work in that sentence. Huffman isn't saying Reddit's data is nice to have. He's saying the models can't be good without it. And he's right — cuz Reddit is the #1 cited source in AI-generated answers across multiple platforms. The models don't just use Reddit data. They depend on it. Every time an AI answers "what's the best budget laptop" or "how do I negotiate a raise," it's drawing on the messy, opinionated, deeply human threads that only exist on Reddit.

From Data Deal to Product Partnership

By Q4 2025, Huffman's framing evolved. The relationship with AI companies wasn't just transactional anymore:

"The conversation is shifting from a purely business deal to more of a product partnership."

This is the signal. Reddit isn't just licensing a static dataset — it's positioning itself as an ongoing supplier of the thing AI models need most: fresh, authentic, human-generated text. Every day, millions of ppl write new posts & comments on Reddit. That continuous flow of human thought is irreplaceable. You can't synthetically generate it. You can't scrape it at scale without legal consequences. You have to buy it from the source.

Stack Overflow figured this out too, but from a position of pain. Traffic fell 50%, they laid off 28% of staff — & then sold their dataset to OpenAI. Revenue grew from $89 million to $125 million. The pivot worked, but it was reactive. Reddit's move was strategic from day one. They saw the leverage before the disruption hit.

The Infrastructure Layer

Zoom out & a bigger pattern emerges. Broadcom's CEO Hock Tan told investors AI represents a "$60 to $90 billion serviceable addressable market" — & that's just for custom AI accelerators & networking. Every model needs two things to function: data & compute infrastructure. The companies that own either are building the picks-and-shovels businesses of the AI gold rush.

Reddit owns the data. Broadcom, Nvidia, & the hyperscalers own the compute. Everyone in between — the companies whose products are built on answers rather than on assets — is in Chegg's position. The question isn't whether AI will use your data. It's whether you have enough leverage to get paid for it.

For PMs, the strategic lesson is counterintuitive but clear: the moat isn't in what your product does — it's in what your product accumulates. Reddit's product is a forum. That's trivially replicable. But two decades of human conversation across every topic imaginable? That's the asset no AI lab can build from scratch. If your product generates unique, high-quality data as a byproduct of its core usage — you're sitting on a gold mine. If it doesn't, you're sitting on a Chegg.
