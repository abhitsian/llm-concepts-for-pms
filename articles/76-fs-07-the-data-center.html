The Data Center

Module 7 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

The data center is no longer a room full of servers. For AI, the data center is the computer. The building, the power supply, the cooling systems, and the networking are all integral parts of one computing system. You don't deploy a model to a server. You deploy it to a building.

Power: The New Constraint

A single AI rack consumes 120 kilowatts. A typical American house uses about 1.5 kilowatts. One rack uses as much power as 80 homes. A medium-sized AI data center runs 10,000–50,000 of these racks. Do the math: that's 1–6 gigawatts of continuous power demand from a single building. For reference, a large nuclear power plant generates about 1 gigawatt.

Power has become the primary constraint on AI scaling in most regions. Companies aren't limited by how many chips they can buy — they're limited by how much electricity they can secure. Google is buying wind farms and gas turbines. Microsoft signed a deal to restart a nuclear reactor at Three Mile Island. Amazon is purchasing data centers near nuclear plants. The AI industry's appetite for electricity is reshaping the energy landscape of entire regions.

About 20 gigawatts of new data center capacity is being deployed in the United States this year alone. For context, that's roughly equivalent to the total electricity consumption of a country like the Netherlands. Power companies that were built to serve residential and light commercial loads are being asked to deliver gigawatts of continuous power to single buildings. The grid wasn't designed for this.

The CapEx Signal

The combined capital expenditure of Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta on AI infrastructure exceeds $600 billion per year. This money pays for land, buildings, turbines, generators, cooling systems, networking equipment, and chips. Most of it is committed 3–4 years in advance.

This is not speculative. You don't sign a $2 billion turbine deposit on a whim. You don't negotiate a 20-year power purchase agreement because you're following a trend. The physical commitments — turbine orders, fab allocations, grid permits — are structural. They take years to build and years to unwind. When critics say "the AI bubble will pop," they need to explain who's going to cancel the turbine orders that have already been placed for 2028 delivery. The infrastructure buildout has its own momentum.

The Hyperscalers vs. The Neoclouds

Hyperscalers (Google, AWS, Azure, Meta) own the data centers, run the GPUs, and rent compute. They have the balance sheets to commit $50–100 billion per year each. They build for their own AI products first, then sell excess capacity. When demand outstrips supply, internal products get priority. This is why Azure sometimes rate-limits external AI customers — Microsoft's own Copilot products consume capacity first.

Neoclouds (CoreWeave, Oracle Cloud, Lambda Labs) rent GPU capacity specifically for AI workloads. They're growing fast: CoreWeave was valued at $35 billion despite being founded in 2017. OpenAI uses CoreWeave and Oracle heavily. The advantage: purpose-built for AI, no legacy enterprise workloads competing for resources. The risk: these are heavily leveraged companies that depend on continued AI demand to service their debt. If demand stalls, the neoclouds face real credit risk.

Dylan Patel's Three Hard Constraints

SemiAnalysis founder Dylan Patel frames AI scaling around three physical constraints, and data centers sit at the intersection of all three:

Logic — TSMC fab capacity and ASML machines determine how many chips can be manufactured. Patel projects this will be the #1 constraint by 2030.

Memory — HBM supply from SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron determines how much data can be fed to those chips. Currently a major bottleneck.

Power — Grid connections, turbines, and land determine how many chips can actually run. This is the most binding constraint right now for new builds.

First Principles Takeaway

When you use an AI product, you are touching a building that consumes more electricity than a small city. The cost of that electricity and the capital cost of that hardware is what you're paying for in your AI subscription or API bill. The $600B annual CapEx from the hyperscalers isn't hype — it's industrial infrastructure with 3–4 year lead times. Your product roadmap should account for the fact that AI capacity is expanding rapidly but is physically constrained in the near term.
