The Data Center

Module 7 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

The data center is no longer a room full of servers. For AI, the data center is the computer. The building, the power supply, the cooling systems, the networking — all integral parts of one computing system. You don't deploy a model to a server. You deploy it to a building.

Power Is the New Constraint

One AI rack: 120 kilowatts. One American house: ~1.5 kilowatts. One rack = 80 houses. A medium-sized AI data center runs 10,000–50,000 racks. That's 1–6 gigawatts of continuous demand from a single facility. A large nuclear plant generates about 1 gigawatt.

About 20 gigawatts of new data center capacity is coming online in the US this year alone — equivalent to the entire electricity consumption of the Netherlands. Power companies built to serve residential loads are being asked to deliver gigawatts of continuous industrial power to single buildings. The grid wasn't designed for this.

Google is buying wind farms and gas turbines. Microsoft signed a deal to restart a nuclear reactor at Three Mile Island. Amazon is purchasing data centers adjacent to nuclear plants. Companies are using diesel truck engines for emergency power. Power is now the primary constraint on AI scaling in most regions — not chips, not software, not models. Electricity.

The $600 Billion Signal

Combined CapEx from Google, Microsoft, Amazon, and Meta on AI infrastructure now exceeds $600 billion per year. Google alone: roughly $75 billion. Microsoft: $80 billion+. This money goes to land, buildings, turbines, generators, cooling, networking, and chips.

Most of it is committed 3–4 years in advance. Turbine deposits for 2028 delivery. Fab capacity booked for 2027 production. Grid permits with 5+ year connection queues. These are physical commitments that take years to build and years to unwind.

This is Patel's mental model #6: "CapEx signals conviction." $600B/yr from the big four isn't hype. You can see it from satellite imagery. The AI buildout is structural, industrial-scale, and irreversible on any short timeline. When critics say "the AI bubble will pop" — they need to explain who cancels the turbine orders already in production.

Hyperscalers vs. Neoclouds

Hyperscalers (Google, AWS, Azure, Meta) own the data centers and GPUs. They build for their own AI products first, then sell excess capacity. When demand outstrips supply, internal products get priority. This is why Azure sometimes rate-limits external AI customers — Microsoft's own Copilot products consume capacity first.

Neoclouds (CoreWeave, Oracle Cloud, Lambda Labs) rent GPU capacity purpose-built for AI. CoreWeave was valued at $35 billion in 2025 despite being founded in 2017. OpenAI uses CoreWeave and Oracle heavily. The advantage: no legacy enterprise workloads competing for resources. The risk: heavily leveraged companies dependent on continued AI demand to service debt. If demand stalls, neoclouds face real credit risk.

Patel's Three Hard Constraints

The data center sits at the intersection of all three physical constraints on AI scaling:

1. Logic — TSMC fab capacity and ASML machines. Patel projects this becomes the #1 constraint by 2030.

2. Memory — HBM supply from three Korean and American companies. Currently a major bottleneck.

3. Power — Grid connections, turbines, land. The most binding constraint right now for new builds.

For product leaders: When you use an AI product, you are touching a building that consumes more electricity than a small city. The cost of that electricity, and the capital cost of that hardware, is what you're paying for in your API bill. Nothing about this is "just software."
