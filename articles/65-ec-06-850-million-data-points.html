850 Million Data Points

Part 6 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

The most staggering AI productivity number of 2024 didn't come from a Silicon Valley startup. It came from a retailer in Bentonville, Arkansas.

Walmart CEO Doug McMillon, Q2 FY2025 earnings call:

"We've used multiple large language models to create or improve more than 850 million pieces of data in our product catalog. Without the use of generative AI, this work would have required nearly 100 times the current headcount to complete in the same amount of time."

850 million. 100x the headcount. From a company that already employs 2.1 million ppl. The math is absurd — without AI, Walmart would've needed tens of thousands of additional catalog workers to accomplish what a fleet of LLMs did. This isn't a pilot program. It's production-scale transformation of the world's largest retail catalog.

Code, Tickets, Messages

Google's Sundar Pichai, Q3 2024:

"More than a quarter of all new code at Google is generated by AI, then reviewed and accepted by engineers."

That figure later rose past 30%. Google — one of the best engineering orgs on earth — now has AI writing a third of its new code. Engineers review & accept it, but the first draft is machine-generated. The implication: developer productivity at Google has structurally shifted, & every company running a large codebase is watching.

Salesforce on Slack AI: summarized over 600 million messages, saving users a collective 1.1 million hours. Slack AI spend grew 50% quarter over quarter. Six hundred million messages distilled into actionable summaries — the kind of grunt work that used to eat entire mornings.

Twilio CEO Khozema Shipchandler went further:

"80% of our new inbound leads are being handled by AI... Our help center assistants have garnered a 75% ticket deflection rate when AI is engaged."

Eighty percent of leads. Seventy-five percent of tickets deflected. Not "we're experimenting." Not "early results are promising." These are operating metrics from a public company reporting to Wall Street.

The Content Multiplier

Airbnb's Brian Chesky on Q4 2024:

"We built a custom AI agent trained on millions of our support interactions. It's already resolving a third of the support issues without needing a live specialist."

One-third of support volume — handled without a human ever touching it. For a company managing millions of listings across 220 countries, that's not a feature. That's an entirely new operating model for customer service.

Duolingo's Luis von Ahn, perhaps the most quotable CEO in edtech:

"With the same number of people, we can make four or five times as much content in the same amount of time."

Four to five times. Same team. Same timeline. Duolingo didn't hire a content army — it gave its existing team AI tools & watched output multiply. The economics of content production fundamentally changed without a single new hire.

Where the ROI Actually Lives

The pattern across all these numbers is striking: the companies seeing the biggest AI productivity gains aren't AI companies. They're companies with massive datasets & repetitive workflows. Retail catalogs. Code reviews. Customer support tickets. Language content. The ROI is clearest where the work was already structured enough for AI to handle — where the task had clear inputs, predictable outputs, & enough historical data to train or prompt against.

This matters for PMs cuz it reframes the AI opportunity. The question isn't "should we build an AI product?" It's "where in our existing workflows is there structured, repetitive work that an LLM could multiply?" Walmart didn't build an AI product. It used AI to fix 850 million catalog entries. Google didn't launch an AI coding startup. It plugged code generation into its existing developer workflow.

The biggest wins aren't new products. They're existing workflows — supercharged. The unsexy, operational, high-volume work that nobody writes blog posts about but every CFO cares about. That's where the 100x lives.
