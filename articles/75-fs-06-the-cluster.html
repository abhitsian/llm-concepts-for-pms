The Cluster

Module 6 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

A single AI chip, no matter how powerful, is not enough. A modern frontier model like Claude or GPT-4 is so large that it doesn't fit in a single chip's memory. The model parameters alone require hundreds of gigabytes — a single H100 has 80GB. Training a frontier model requires not one chip but tens of thousands, running in tight coordination for weeks or months.

Why Communication Matters More Than Compute

The way this works is called a cluster. Thousands of GPUs are placed in racks, and the racks are connected to each other through specialized high-speed networking. During training, the model is split across thousands of chips. Each chip holds a portion of the model and a portion of the training data. They constantly communicate — sending partial results, gradients, and activations back and forth.

The communication between chips is surprisingly critical. If chip A finishes its calculation and has to wait 1 millisecond for chip B to catch up, and you have 10,000 chips, that delay cascades. A 1% inefficiency in network design can make a cluster 20% slower overall. The math is brutal: in a synchronized training run, the slowest chip determines the speed of the entire system.

NVLink and InfiniBand

NVLink connects GPUs within a single rack. Nvidia's GB200 NVL72 rack uses NVLink to wire 72 GPUs together with 1.8 terabytes per second of bisection bandwidth. At this speed, the 72 chips can function as a single massive processor — they share data so fast that the software barely notices they're separate physical devices. This is why Nvidia is becoming a rack company, not a chip company. The chip without the interconnect is like an engine without a drivetrain.

InfiniBand connects racks to each other across the data center. Nvidia acquired Mellanox (the InfiniBand leader) in 2020 for $7 billion — a move that looked expensive at the time and now looks like the bargain of the decade. InfiniBand provides the ultra-low latency that LLM training requires for the all-to-all communication patterns between racks. The alternative — standard Ethernet — is cheaper but slower. For frontier model training, where microseconds of latency multiply across billions of operations, InfiniBand dominates.

The Scale

Training GPT-4 required roughly 25,000 A100 GPUs running for approximately 100 days. Current frontier models use 50,000–100,000 chips. The next generation will use more. Each of these chips consumes hundreds of watts. The networking equipment consumes additional power. The cooling systems to keep all of this from overheating consume still more.

A medium-sized AI training cluster occupies a building the size of a warehouse, draws tens of megawatts of power, and costs $1–5 billion to build. The cluster is not a collection of servers. The cluster is the computer. A single training run orchestrates all of these chips as one coherent system, synchronized to within microseconds, for months on end. If a single chip fails during training, the entire run must checkpoint, replace the failed chip, and resume. Hardware reliability at this scale is as much an engineering challenge as the AI itself.

The Cooling Revolution

Air-cooled server racks top out at 20–30 kilowatts per rack. AI racks run at 100+ kilowatts. Air simply cannot remove heat fast enough at this density. The industry is moving to liquid cooling — pumping chilled water or specialized coolant directly through cold plates mounted on each chip. Some designs use immersion cooling, submerging entire servers in tanks of non-conductive fluid.

Data center design is being rebuilt from scratch around thermal reality. Buildings that were designed for air-cooled servers cannot accommodate liquid-cooled AI racks without major renovation. New AI data centers are designed liquid-first, with plumbing as important as electrical wiring.

First Principles Takeaway

The cluster is where individual chips become useful. The chip determines raw compute capability, but the network determines how much of that capability you can actually use. Nvidia's dominance isn't just about making better chips — it's about controlling the interconnect (NVLink + InfiniBand) that makes clusters work. When you hear about "GPU utilization" problems, the root cause is usually networking, not the chip itself.
