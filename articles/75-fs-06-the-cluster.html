The Cluster

Module 6 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

A single AI chip, no matter how powerful, is not enough. A frontier model like Claude or GPT-4 doesn't fit in one chip's memory. The model parameters alone require hundreds of gigabytes — a single H100 has 80GB. Training a frontier model requires 10,000–100,000 GPUs running in tight coordination for weeks to months. One chip is nothing. The cluster is the computer.

Why Communication > Compute

Thousands of GPUs sit in racks. Racks connect to each other through specialized high-speed networking. During training, the model is split across thousands of chips. Each holds a portion of the model and data. They constantly communicate — sending partial results, gradients, activations back and forth in patterns called all-reduce and all-to-all.

The communication is surprisingly critical. If chip A finishes its calculation and waits 1 millisecond for chip B to catch up, and you have 10,000 chips, that delay cascades. A 1% inefficiency in network design can make a cluster 20% slower overall. In a synchronized training run, the slowest chip determines the speed of the entire system. One bad cable, one misconfigured switch, one overheated link — and 10,000 chips sit idle waiting.

NVLink and InfiniBand

NVLink connects GPUs within a rack. The GB200 NVL72 uses NVLink to wire 72 GPUs together with 1.8 terabytes per second of bisection bandwidth. At this speed, the 72 chips function as a single processor. The software barely notices they're separate physical devices.

InfiniBand connects racks to each other across the data center. Nvidia acquired Mellanox (the InfiniBand leader) in 2020 for $7 billion — a price that looked steep at the time and now looks like the bargain of the decade. InfiniBand provides the ultra-low latency that LLM training requires for communication between racks. Standard Ethernet is cheaper but adds microseconds of latency. For frontier model training, where those microseconds multiply across billions of operations, InfiniBand dominates.

This is why Nvidia's networking business is as strategically important as its GPU business. The chip without the interconnect is an engine without a drivetrain. Nvidia sells the engine and the drivetrain and the highway system.

The Scale of a Training Run

Training GPT-4 required roughly 25,000 A100 GPUs running for approximately 100 days. Current frontier models use 50,000–100,000 chips. Each consumes hundreds of watts. The networking equipment consumes additional power. The cooling systems to keep it from overheating consume still more.

A medium-sized AI training cluster occupies a building the size of a warehouse, draws tens of megawatts, and costs $1–5 billion to build. If a single chip fails during training, the entire run must checkpoint, replace the failed hardware, and resume from the last save. Hardware reliability at this scale is as much an engineering challenge as the AI itself.

The Cooling Revolution

Air-cooled server racks top out at 20–30 kilowatts. AI racks run 100–120 kilowatts. Air simply cannot remove heat fast enough. The industry is moving to liquid cooling — chilled water or specialized coolant pumped directly through cold plates on each chip. Some designs use immersion cooling: entire servers submerged in tanks of non-conductive fluid.

New AI data centers are designed liquid-first, with plumbing as important as electrical wiring. Buildings designed for air-cooled servers cannot accommodate AI racks without major renovation. Electrician wages have doubled in data-center-heavy regions. The physical buildout of AI is, in significant part, a plumbing and electrical problem.
