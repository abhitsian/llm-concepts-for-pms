Training

Module 9 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

A neural network before training is a vast web of random numbers. Billions of them. These numbers — called weights or parameters — encode nothing. After training, they encode language, logic, facts, reasoning patterns, and something that looks remarkably like understanding. Training is the process that transforms random noise into intelligence. Nobody fully understands why it works.

The Deceptively Simple Idea

Take a large fraction of human writing — books, websites, code, conversations, scientific papers, legislation, poetry, Reddit threads. Show the model a sequence of text. Ask: what word comes next? It guesses. It's wrong. Adjust the billions of numbers slightly to make it less wrong next time. Repeat. Two trillion times.

This sounds almost insultingly simple. The emergent result of doing this at sufficient scale is a system that can write working code, reason through multi-step math, summarize thousand-page legal documents, translate between 100+ languages, and generate plausible business strategy memos. The scaling laws — discovered empirically at OpenAI in 2020 — showed that loss (how wrong the model's predictions are) decreases as a smooth power law with model size, dataset size, and compute. More of each = predictably better. We ran the experiment and it worked. The theory explaining why is still incomplete.

The Numbers

Training a frontier model costs $50 million to $1+ billion. It requires 10,000–100,000 GPUs synchronized for weeks to months. The electricity bill alone runs into tens of millions. Training data: trillions of tokens — a significant fraction of all publicly available human text, plus licensed datasets, synthetic data, and curated collections.

Training happens once. The result is a fixed set of billions of numbers — the model weights — that encode everything the model knows. This is CapEx: a one-time investment that produces an asset. The model you call through an API today was trained months ago.

Post-Training: Where Behavior Comes From

The base model from pre-training is capable but raw. It knows enormous amounts but doesn't follow instructions well, hallucinates confidently, can be harmful, and lacks the conversational polish users expect. Post-training shapes raw capability into useful behavior.

RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback): Human raters evaluate pairs of model outputs — which is better? More helpful? More accurate? More harmless? The model learns to generate outputs humans prefer. Over many iterations, it develops the helpful, safety-aware behavior that characterizes Claude and ChatGPT.

Reasoning is the current frontier. Models like o3, DeepSeek-R1, and Claude's extended thinking mode are trained via reinforcement learning to generate long chains of internal reasoning before answering. They explore approaches, backtrack, verify their work. The result: dramatically better performance on complex problems — math, code, multi-step logic. The cost: 5–50x more tokens generated per query, because the model "thinks" internally before producing a visible answer.

This is Patel's mental model #3: "Training = CapEx. Inference = OpEx." Train once, serve forever — but serving is where the money burns. The shift from pre-training scale to post-training sophistication is the current strategic inflection. Patel says "we're in early innings of post-training." The techniques for shaping model behavior are less mature and improving faster than raw pre-training.
