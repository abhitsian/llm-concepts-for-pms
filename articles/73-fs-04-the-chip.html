Graphics Processing Unit. The name is an accident of history. GPUs were designed to render video games — millions of similar calculations per frame, one per pixel. It turned out that training neural networks also requires millions of similar calculations. The GPU was the wrong tool that became the right tool, and Nvidia rode that accident to a $3+ trillion market cap.

Parallelism: Why GPUs Win

A CPU — the processor in your laptop — is designed to do one complex task very fast. A few powerful cores that handle intricate logic, branching decisions, irregular data flows. Think of a brilliant professor who can solve any problem, but only one at a time.

A GPU has thousands of smaller, simpler cores that all execute the same operation in parallel on different data. Think of an army of calculators, each doing basic arithmetic, but all working simultaneously. Neural network math is almost entirely matrix multiplication: multiply this row by that column, billions of times. This is embarrassingly parallel — every multiplication is independent. GPUs are built for exactly this.

Nvidia's B200 Blackwell chip delivers 4.5 petaflops of FP8 compute. That's 4,500 trillion floating-point operations per second. On a single chip.

The CUDA Moat

Nvidia didn't invent the GPU. But they built CUDA — the software layer that made GPUs programmable for general computation. CUDA launched in 2006. For 20 years, every AI framework (PyTorch, TensorFlow, JAX), every optimization library, every production deployment, every PhD thesis has been written against CUDA.

AMD makes GPUs with comparable raw hardware specs. They don't have 20 years of software ecosystem. Every researcher who wrote a breakthrough paper used CUDA. Every startup that shipped an AI product used CUDA. The graduate student who'll invent next year's architecture will use CUDA.

The chip is not the moat. The ecosystem around the chip is the moat. Hardware can be matched in 3–4 years. Software ecosystems take decades. This is Dylan Patel's mental model #2: "Software is the real moat. AMD has great hardware. Nvidia wins because CUDA took 15 years to build. You can't clone a software ecosystem."

The Alternatives

Google's TPU (Tensor Processing Unit) is purpose-built for the specific matrix math neural networks need. Less flexible than a GPU, but faster and cheaper per operation for AI. Google doesn't sell TPUs — they use them internally and rent access through Cloud. TPUs power Gemini and much of Claude's inference. The advantage: Google co-designs the chip, the compiler, the networking, and the model architecture as one integrated system. Patel's mental model #1: "Systems > microarchitecture. Google's TPU wins not because the chip spec is better, but because the whole system is co-designed."

Amazon's Trainium follows the same logic: custom silicon optimized for AI, available only through AWS. The pitch: 30–40% better price-performance for supported workloads. The tradeoff: narrower software support, less flexibility.

The pattern: every hyperscaler is building custom chips to reduce Nvidia dependence. The GPU remains the default — the thing that works for everything. Custom silicon wins on efficiency for known, stable workloads. The GPU wins on flexibility for everything else.

The Rack Is the New Chip

Nvidia's latest product isn't a GPU. It's a rack. The GB200 NVL72 is 72 GPUs connected via NVLink so tightly they behave as a single massive processor. It consumes 120 kilowatts of power — 80 houses' worth. Jensen Huang doesn't sell you a chip anymore. He sells you a data center building block.

This is Patel's observation playing out: Nvidia is becoming a systems company, not a chip company. The chip, the interconnect, the networking, the software — sold as one integrated rack. The margin on the system is higher than the margin on the chip alone. And the switching cost for the system is much higher than for a single component.
