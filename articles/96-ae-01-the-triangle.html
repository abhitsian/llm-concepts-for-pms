The Triangle

Part 1 of Field Notes: AI Economics series
╌╌╌╌

Every previous tech super-cycle ended the same way. The bottom of the stack, where the capex was spent, looked enormous in the early innings — cables, fiber, fabs, base stations, server farms. Then the application layer flipped it. By the time the cycle stabilized, application-layer revenue dwarfed infrastructure-layer revenue. The internet ended in a triangle pointing up. Mobile ended in a triangle pointing up. Cloud ended in a triangle pointing up.

AI, four years in, has the opposite shape. The triangle points down.

This is the central thesis of the Stanford AI Economics course Apoorv Agrawal of Altimeter is teaching this term. He opened the first session by asking the room why nobody had a good explanation for it.

The Numbers Behind the Shape

Three layers, very different gross margins. Read top-down, from the most profitable to the least:

  • Semiconductors: roughly 75% gross margins at the data-center revenue line. Concentrated. NVIDIA captures most of it.
  • Infrastructure / cloud: middle. Hyperscalers and inference providers. The most competitive layer in the stack.
  • Applications: 0–30% gross margins, depending on who you ask. Cursor still not profitable at billions of revenue. ChatGPT subsidized at every consumer tier.

Compare that to cloud, which the same chart shows ending up almost the inverse: thin gross margins on the AWS bare-metal layer, fat margins on Snowflake and ServiceNow and the rest of the modern application stack.

If you map revenue and profit onto a triangle, AI today looks like every dollar of value is concentrated where the silicon is melted, not where the user is touching the product.

Why It Looks This Way

The simplest explanation a student offered — "NVIDIA has a monopoly so they can charge it" — is partially right. But it is not the whole story. The deeper answer Agrawal pushed the room toward is the per-user marginal cost.

"Software ate the world because I could build software, distribute it to millions of people, and the marginal cost of running it was close to zero. These software businesses ran at 80, even 90 percent gross margins. That is not the case with AI. The incremental user of an AI application is not marginally free. It's actually quite a bit more expensive, because it turns out you've got to burn those GPUs."

Cloud-era SaaS distributed bits with a fixed cost. AI applications distribute tokens with a per-unit cost. Each chat. Each agent loop. Each reasoning trace. The incremental user of Cursor or Perplexity comes with a meter running. The 90% gross margin pattern of the last era is structurally unavailable to the new one.

The CapEx Side

The other half of the triangle is what makes the picture so striking. Even with AI's profit pool concentrated at the top, the capex investment in the bottom layers is the largest in commercial history. Brad Gerstner introduced the comparison from this same lecture series:

"This is the CapEx spend by the five hyperscalers on AI. To put this in context, this is one of the biggest investments we are making — bigger than space, bigger than the highway system, the Manhattan Project. Second only to the US defense budget."

Bigger than space, bigger than highways, bigger than the Manhattan Project — in a single year, in 2025. The 2026 collective figure from Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle is reportedly $660–690B, of which roughly 75% is directly tied to AI. Roughly 100% of operating cash flow.

How Long Does the Triangle Stay Inverted?

The honest answer Agrawal gave is "longer than I thought."

"My guess is that it might stay this way for longer than I anticipated. In the cloud, that range is about a decade. I have a feeling this might stay this way longer, because of just how hard it is to get the substrate."

Two catalysts could break the equilibrium. First, a successful breakout from a hyperscaler ASIC program — Google's TPU is the precedent, with Meta MTIA and various others coming. Second, hyperscalers reducing their capex guidance, which would be the loudest possible signal that the current spending pace cannot sustain itself.

Until one of those happens, the triangle stays where it is, and so does the math: every dollar made on AI passes through an NVIDIA chip on the way to the user.
