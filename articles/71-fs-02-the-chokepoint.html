The Chokepoint

Module 2 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

There is exactly one company in the world that makes the machines required to manufacture leading-edge AI chips. It's called ASML. It's in Veldhoven, the Netherlands. It makes about 50 of these machines per year. Each one costs $350 million, takes 40 shipping containers to transport, and requires 90,000 components sourced from 5,000 suppliers in 24 countries.

Let that land. One company, 50 machines per year, $350 million each. Every AI model you've ever used — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — exists because of a supply chain that runs through a single factory in a Dutch town of 45,000 people.

What EUV Actually Does

The machines are called EUV lithography systems. EUV stands for Extreme Ultraviolet — light with a wavelength of 13.5 nanometers, far shorter than visible light. This short wavelength is what allows the machines to etch transistor patterns that are only a few nanometers wide.

Generating EUV light is absurdly complex. A high-powered laser fires at tiny droplets of molten tin, 50,000 times per second. Each hit creates a plasma that emits EUV photons. These photons are collected by mirrors polished to atomic-level smoothness — the flattest objects ever manufactured. The light is then directed through a series of masks to project circuit patterns onto a silicon wafer coated in photosensitive material.

The entire process happens inside a vacuum chamber, because EUV light is absorbed by air. The precision required is equivalent to hitting a golf ball from the moon and landing it in a specific cup on a specific hole on a specific golf course on Earth. And doing it 50,000 times per second.

Why There's Only One

ASML didn't stumble into monopoly. The company spent 20 years and tens of billions of dollars developing EUV technology, through periods when most of the industry assumed it would never work. The key components — the light source (made by Cymer/Trumpf), the mirrors (made by Zeiss), the precision stages — each represent decades of specialized R&D by companies that have no competitors at this level of performance.

No Chinese company can make an EUV machine. No American company can. No other European company can. The knowledge is distributed across thousands of specialists, and the integration expertise — making all 90,000 components work together at atomic precision — lives inside ASML. You cannot replicate this with money alone. You need the institutional knowledge that only exists in the people who built the last generation of machines.

The Geopolitics

This makes ASML the most geopolitically important company most people have never heard of. The United States has pressured the Netherlands to restrict ASML's sales to China. China cannot manufacture leading-edge AI chips without EUV machines. This single chokepoint — a factory in the Netherlands — is a lever that shapes which countries can build frontier AI systems.

Taiwan's TSMC makes 90% of the world's leading-edge chips using ASML's machines. If either ASML or TSMC were disrupted — by natural disaster, conflict, or policy — the entire global AI buildout would stall. Not slow down. Stall. There is no backup. There is no alternative supplier. The lead time to build a comparable capability from scratch is measured in decades, not years.

First Principles Takeaway

The AI industry's most critical dependency is not a software library, a model architecture, or a dataset. It's a physical machine made by a single company in the Netherlands. This chokepoint means AI scaling is governed by industrial capacity, not by ideas. Understanding this changes how you think about AI timelines, because the constraint isn't "can we design a better model?" — it's "can we physically manufacture enough chips to run it?"
