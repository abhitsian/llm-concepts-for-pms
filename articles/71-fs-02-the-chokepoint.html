The Chokepoint

Module 2 of From Sand to Software
┈┈┈┈

There is exactly one company in the world that makes the machines required to manufacture leading-edge AI chips. It's called ASML. It's in Veldhoven, the Netherlands — a town of 45,000 people. It makes about 50 of these machines per year. Each one costs $350 million, takes 40 shipping containers to transport, and requires 90,000 components sourced from 5,000 suppliers in 24 countries.

Let that land. One company, 50 machines per year, $350 million each. Every AI model you've ever used — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama — exists because of a supply chain that runs through a single factory in a Dutch town most people have never heard of.

This is not a technology story. This is a geopolitics story.

What EUV Actually Does

The machines are called EUV lithography systems — Extreme Ultraviolet. They use light with a wavelength of 13.5 nanometers, far shorter than visible light, to project circuit patterns onto silicon wafers. The shorter the wavelength, the smaller the features you can etch. Smaller features = more transistors per chip = more compute per dollar.

Generating EUV light is absurdly complex. A high-powered CO2 laser fires at tiny droplets of molten tin, 50,000 times per second. Each hit creates a plasma that emits EUV photons. These photons are collected by mirrors polished to atomic-level smoothness — made by Zeiss, they're the flattest objects ever manufactured by humans. The light is then directed through a series of masks to project circuit patterns onto a photosensitive coating on the wafer.

The entire process happens inside a vacuum chamber, because EUV light is absorbed by air. The precision required is equivalent to using a laser to write on a football from 15 kilometers away. And doing it 50,000 times per second, on a wafer spinning at high speed, for 24 hours straight, with defect rates measured in parts per billion.

Why There's Only One

ASML didn't stumble into monopoly. The company spent 20 years and tens of billions of dollars developing EUV technology, through periods when most of the semiconductor industry assumed it would never work. The key components — the light source (Cymer/Trumpf), the mirrors (Zeiss), the precision stages — each represent decades of specialized R&D by companies that have no competitors at this level of performance.

No Chinese company can make an EUV machine. No American company can. No other European company can. The knowledge is distributed across thousands of specialists, and the integration expertise — making all 90,000 components work together at atomic precision — lives inside ASML. You cannot replicate this with money alone. You need the institutional knowledge that only exists in the people who built the last generation of machines.

The Geopolitics

The United States has pressured the Netherlands to restrict ASML's sales to China. Without EUV machines, China cannot manufacture chips below ~7 nanometers — which means it cannot build frontier AI chips domestically. This single chokepoint is a lever that determines which nations can build frontier AI systems and which cannot.

Meanwhile, Taiwan's TSMC makes 90% of the world's leading-edge chips using ASML's machines. If either ASML or TSMC were disrupted — by conflict, natural disaster, or policy change — the entire global AI buildout would stall. Not slow down. Stall. There is no backup. There is no alternative supplier. The lead time to build a comparable capability from scratch is measured in decades.

Whoever controls access to these machines controls the future of AI. That's not hyperbole. It's physics.
