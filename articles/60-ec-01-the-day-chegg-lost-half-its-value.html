The Day Chegg Lost Half Its Value

Part 1 of The Earnings Call series
╌╌╌╌

May 1, 2023. Chegg's Q1 earnings call. The stock is trading around $9, already battered from its $115 pandemic peak. CEO Dan Rosensweig gets on the line & delivers six sentences that will become the most cited AI disruption moment in public market history.

"In the first part of the year, we saw no noticeable impact from ChatGPT on our new account growth and we were meeting expectations on new sign-ups. However, since March we saw a significant spike in student interest in ChatGPT. We now believe it's having an impact on our new customer growth rate."

The stock crashed 48% in a single after-hours session. Not over weeks. Not over months. In minutes. By the time markets opened the next morning, half the company's value had evaporated cuz a CEO said three words out loud that every investor was already thinking: ChatGPT is winning.

The Full Collapse

The numbers that followed were brutal. Subscribers dropped 31% year-over-year. Chegg's market cap — which had peaked at $14.5 billion during the homework-help boom of 2021 — fell below $200 million. The company laid off 45% of its workforce. Rosensweig, in a later interview, offered the most painfully honest self-assessment any CEO has ever given about AI disruption:

"I became the poster child for getting your ass kicked in the public markets by A.I., since I lost 40% of value in five minutes."

What made Chegg so vulnerable wasn't that it was a bad company. It was that its entire product was the answer. Students didn't come to Chegg for community, for credentials, for the experience of learning. They came for the answer to problem 47b. And when a free, instant, always-available answer machine showed up — one that could explain its reasoning & rephrase at any level — the value proposition collapsed overnight. The product was commoditized by something that did the same thing, better, for free.

The Company That Sold Its Data to Its Killers

Stack Overflow tells the mirror-image story. Traffic fell roughly 50% after ChatGPT launched. The company laid off 28% of its staff. The same pattern as Chegg — ppl stopped visiting cuz the AI already had the answers. But then Stack Overflow did something Chegg couldn't: it pivoted.

Stack Overflow sold its data to OpenAI. The corpus of 58 million questions & answers — the very dataset that had trained the models killing its traffic — became a licensed product. Revenue grew from $89 million to $125 million. Not by fighting AI, but by feeding it. The company that sold its data to its killers, & got paid handsomely for it.

The difference between Chegg & Stack Overflow is structural. Stack Overflow's value was in the corpus — the accumulated knowledge of millions of developers, irreplaceable & continuously growing. Chegg's value was in the delivery — serving up answers that already existed in textbooks. When a better delivery mechanism appeared, Chegg had nothing left to sell. Stack Overflow had the raw material the new delivery mechanism needed to function.

The Lesson for Every PM

Every product manager building in the AI era should study Chegg the way business school students study Blockbuster. The lesson isn't "AI is coming for you" — that's too vague to be useful. The lesson is specific: if your product IS the answer, you are maximally exposed to a better answer machine.

The products that survived — & even thrived — were the ones where the answer was a byproduct of something deeper. Reddit survived cuz the conversation was the product, not the conclusion. Stack Overflow survived cuz the corpus was the asset, not the page view. Wikipedia survived cuz its mission isn't monetized in a way that AI can undercut.

Chegg had none of those buffers. It charged students for answers. ChatGPT gave them answers for free. That's not a competitive challenge. That's an extinction event. Rosensweig saw it in real time, said it out loud, & the market repriced his company in five minutes. The fastest disruption story ever told — on an earnings call.
